An Experimental Physicist's Lesson

It was today I had my lab examination of an experiment that I had performed over the last 6 weeks. I was confident of my preparation for the viva and the demonstration. I believed I had covered all corners and gave it all the effort I could have.
My examiner walked into the room and asked a colleague (who had a similar experiment) some questions, all of which I knew the answers to. My turn was next and I felt good and calm. But when I introduced him to my experiment and we had some discussions over it, he asked me one question: "If I increase the slit width, why do the spectral lines bulge/widen?" It was a simple yet an important question that I failed to answer. To which he subtly replied "Think about it" and walked away. But think I could not, I went into a free fall for another couple of hours, filled with sadness over the fact that I couldn't answer an important question of an experiment I had been doing for 6 weeks. The question was practically there the whole time! The setup was simple and all I had to do was take a good look at the setup and ask why is this slit even there here? On the abstract I knew why, it seemed trivial then, but when I was asked to go deep into the why? I failed to do so, mainly because I didn't give it much importance during the 6 weeks I had.
An experimentalist is one who first looks at his/her setup and knows where his/her errors would creep in, and focus on imporoving there. He/she would know the limits of the results one can obtain with their setup. Pushing it to great lengths is another topic altogether.
An experimentalist is also one who tackles the simple but basic/important questions first. Once the conceptually crucial questions are sorted, he/she can proceed to the next step. Read on what you need to do first! and then start/jump to the fun experiments. Read and then play with questions, there should be a reason for every piece of your setup, why is it there and what does it help in.

Happy learning! And until the next experimental screw up,
Yours,
Danush S, 3 February, 2021
